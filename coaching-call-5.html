The trap

Selling "websites" =
commodity

If you sell a website, you compete with every freelancer, template and $500 cousin-with-a-laptop. The only lever left is price, and that's a race to the bottom.

A commodity competes on price. A mechanism competes on value.
The real problem in the niche

The trust recession

In most niches every business says the exact same thing. "Locally owned, 5-star, family operated, certified." They've commoditised themselves.

When they all sound the same,
the customer chooses on price.
Roofing is the example. The pattern repeats in almost every niche.

Your #1 job isn't design or code. It's to help the business win the trust of their customer so they get chosen every time, and stop competing on price.

What the blueprint is

The mechanism

It's not the fact that you "do web design." It's that you deliver value through a specific strategy. That strategy, documented, is the blueprint.

  • →A repeatable strategy uncovered in your research, not a one-off design.
  • →Backed by results you can show on screen.
  • →Makes you a specialist, not a generic designer.
From strategy to something they can see

Make the blueprint
tangible

The strategy doc you built in the research phase becomes two things they can actually look at, and the proposal is what sells the blueprint.

Strategy doc→ Live homepage (~$5, Website Factory)→ Proposal that sells the strategy

The proposal isn't the strategy. It's the strategy wrapped in sales: removing risk, what they get, and how you deliver it with your blueprint.

Why the price holds

not time for money

A freelancer sells hours. A niche specialist with a proven mechanism sells an outcome. That's why the same deliverable can be $3k or $8k. The difference is the mechanism behind it.

The shift
Stop selling your time.
Start selling the result.

How you find the number

Price = the value of
solving the problem

You don't pull a price from the air. On the call you learn how much they make and what a customer is worth, so you can size the value of solving their problem, then price against that.

What's a new client worth to them?

Make the price obvious

The ROI math

High-ticket B2C is the cheat code: one or two extra jobs pays for the entire project. Do the math with them, on their numbers.

Avg job value × a few extra jobs
= your fee paid back fast
→ everything after is profit for them.
When the return dwarfs the price, the price stops being the conversation.
The vehicle · beginner-friendly

The two-call close

The simplest, most ethical way to sell: you don't pitch, you consult. Two calls, and you sell their own problem back to them as the solution.

Call 1

Discovery

Ask the right questions. Find their goal and their problem. Then: "I'll go research and come back with a solution."

Call 2

Solution

Present the strategy that solves it, wrapped in a proposal. Then close.

Call 1 · Discovery

Shut up & listen

Call one is for gathering information, not selling. If you're talking a lot, you're losing.

  • →Talk 10%, they talk 90%. Open with "Tell me about your business."
  • →Gather who / how / what / how much, including what they make, so you can size the value.
  • →Use their exact verbiage and repeat it back. They feel understood.
  • →Record the call and take notes. Don't assume you know the problem.
Call 2 · Present & close

Sell their problem
back to them

  • 01Recap the goal & the problem in their words. "It sounds like you know my business better than I do."
  • 02Show the research. 2 to 3 winners in the niche and what they do differently.
  • 03Present the strategy (your blueprint) as the solution to their problem.
  • 04Wrap it in the proposal and move to close.
What the blueprint unlocks

The one-call close

Once you have a proven blueprint, product/market fit, and a live homepage + proposal ready before the call, you don't need two calls. You close on the first one.

"One-call closes all day.
Money on the call."
The two-call close is your training wheels. The blueprint is what takes them off.
Revealing the price

Stack, then reveal

  • 01Answer every question first. No open questions before the price.
  • 02Isolate the price: "Other than price, any reason we wouldn't work together?"
  • 03Stack the value, then reveal the number.
  • 04Then go quiet. First person to speak loses.
The positioning line

"Most affordable
at my level."

Even at premium prices you can truthfully say this, because you did the research, built the strategy, and uncovered how to bring value. You're not the cheapest; you're the best value at your tier.

Charge more by fulfilling cheaply with the stack, and selling the value.
De-risk the buyer

Reverse the risk

Premium price needs the risk off the table. The live-homepage-first process makes that safe for you, too. They've already seen it before money changes hands.

Offer

Free upfront

They see the live homepage first; if they love it, they pay. ~$5 to produce, so you carry almost no risk.

Terms

One-time offer

Small deposit to start, then a payment plan. Zero-risk entry for them, secured cash for you.

De-risk the client

Your risk-reversal

A satisfaction guarantee is your strongest closing tool. It removes the client's last bit of hesitation and proves your confidence. And the way you fulfil makes it safe to offer.

  • →What it is. You stand behind the work. If they're not happy with the design, they're not locked in.
  • →Why it's safe for you. They approve the live homepage before you build out the full site, so you never invest in work they haven't signed off on (~$5 to produce it).
  • →How to position it. Lead with the confidence: "I'm so sure you'll love it that there's no risk to you."
The guarantee isn't a cost. It's what makes a premium price land.
