Step 1 · Niche (1 of 2)

Pick your niche

Niching down lets you specialise, charge premium, and become "the guy." First, judge the money side:

  • →Ticket size first. One client should be able to pay for the whole project — and a second makes you both profitable. High-ticket beats high-volume.
  • →Industry size, but don't obsess. Big enough to have room, not so big it's saturated. A smaller "blue ocean" can still print.
  • →Recession proof? Will they still spend when things tighten? (The trampoline park shut down in COVID.)
  • →Already done work there? Existing momentum is a strong signal.
Step 1 · Niche (2 of 2)

Pick your niche

  • →B2C over B2B. Consumer-facing businesses actually use the website to win trust and customers — so a great site is worth far more to them.
  • →Standardised blueprint. Are the businesses similar enough to systemise? (Window cleaning = yes. "Insurance" = too many different sub-types.)
  • →Who benefits most from a website? High-commitment, "serious" decisions where the customer researches before buying — surgeons, hair transplants, roofing, dentists, med spas.
  • →Cons? Watch for heavy compliance/legal jargon (legal, insurance, medical) — still money there, just more friction.
▶ Do it now · by hand

Build your niche scorecard

  • 01Open a Google Sheet. Put 5 candidate niches down the rows.
  • 02Add a column per factor: worked in it? · ticket size · industry size · recession-proof? · B2C? · standardised? · cons.
  • 03Score each niche honestly across the row.
  • 04Shortlist your top 3 — those are what you'll research next.
Systemise later · Claude can score & compare niches for you — but do it by hand first so you understand the trade-offs.

Step 2 · Competitor research

Study the winners

Look at the agencies already winning in your niche. They're "just" web designers — but what they actually sell is a strategy that delivers value. Your job is to uncover that blueprint and replicate it.

On the competitor

How they operate

What they price, how they position, where they're niche vs. generic.

On their website

What earns trust

Trust badges, reviews with counts, case studies (situation → solution → result), CTAs & friction.

Watch for the big tell: are they selling features, or results? Sell results.
▶ Do it now · by hand

The agency teardown

  • 01Google "[your niche] website design agency" (e.g. "dentist website design agency").
  • 02Open a Google Doc. List 5–10 agencies, with 5–10 bullets each: pros, cons, approach.
  • 03For each site ask: trust badges? reviews with counts + rating? case studies? frictionless contact? features or results?
  • 04Pick the best to model — and note what you'd do better. Dig into their case studies to see the repeatable winning layout.
Systemise later · The Website Factory scrapes & scores competitors with Apify + Claude Vision — learn to read a site with your own eyes first.
Step 3 · Market research

niche hang out?

You can't fish where there are no fish. Find the watering hole before you write a single message — different demographics live in different places.

Professional niches

▶ Do it now · by hand

Find & log the groups

  • 01On Facebook, search "[niche] owners" (e.g. "dental owners" → The Dental Network, 138k members).
  • 02Open every promising group. Is it popping? Check member count & posts per day.
  • 03Log each one in your master sheet, with the platform it's on.

Step 4 · Verbiage

They don't want
a website

Underneath the surface ask is the real goal — and the words they use to describe it. Capture that language; you'll mirror it back in your offer and on calls.

The surface ask

"A new website"

Nice design, modern look, mobile-friendly.

The real goal

Trust & customers

Be chosen over competitors. More booked jobs. Look like the obvious safe choice.

Their exact phrases become your headlines later.
▶ Do it now · by hand

Capture their verbiage

  • 01Read the group posts and the reviews (theirs & competitors'). Copy the exact words, pains & goals into your sheet.
  • 02Listen for the patterns: "SEO never worked," "paid an agency, no results," "don't know who to trust."
  • 03DM or call a few owners and ask straight up: what's your biggest headache?
  • 04Jump on as many calls as you can — every conversation sharpens your read of the niche.
Systemise later · The Content Engine mines reviews & calls for voice-of-customer — but the words you collect by hand become your first headlines.

Step 6 · The offer

Build an irresistible offer

The offer changes with your stage of business. Early on, you carry the risk to win trust; as your proof grows, you shift the risk back to them.

Stage 1Free (portfolio + experience) → Stage 2Free upfront (pay if you like it) → Stage 3Deposit down → Stage 4Payment plan → Stage 5Raise prices
Reference: Alex Hormozi, "$100M Offers."
The beginner power move

The free-upfront offer

You build a live homepage and have the proposal ready before you ever get on the call — they only pay if they love it. It feels insane to the prospect, which is exactly why it cuts through the spam.

Why it works

  • →Removes all risk from the prospect.
  • →Proves your confidence and seriousness.
  • →It's tangible — a real, live homepage, not a mockup.

Why it's safe for you

  • →~$5 to generate a fully functional live homepage before the call (Website Factory).
  • →No developer until it's sold.
  • →Small risk, huge upside.
Goal of the offer = sell the call, not the website.
Anatomy

Keep it short, hyper-personal, and human — never a wall of text.

Deep dive · Abou's gold

The cold call

Lowest cost, fastest feedback, most reps with real prospects. The goal of the call is not to sell a website — it's to sell the next step.

DM / Call → sell the call → Call → sell the Zoom → Zoom → sell the website

Move them along the natural progression of trust. Don't rush steps. Every prospect you talk to teaches you the niche — even the ones who don't buy.
